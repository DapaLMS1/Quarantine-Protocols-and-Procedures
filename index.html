Quarantine Management

Quarantine is a critical aspect of infection control, acting as a "firewall" when there is a known or suspected risk of high-virulence pathogens.

How to interact: Hover over (or tap) the solid colored boxes below to reveal information.
Quarantine is required if instruments are used on a patient with a known infectious disease or in an emergency where a patient’s status is unknown but symptoms suggest high risk.

When to Initiate

Purpose

The primary objective is to physically and procedurally isolate "at-risk" equipment from the general instrument pool, minimizing the risk of cross-contamination.
Protocols are typically reserved for virulent agents like Creutzfeldt-Jakob Disease (CJD). Prions—the causative agents of CJD—are not destroyed by standard sterilisation cycles (134°C for 3.5 minutes) and require specialized intervention.

High-Risk Microorganisms

For Quarantined Loads. When a quarantine event is triggered, the following steps must be followed:

Immediate Isolation

Instruments must be placed in a separate, puncture-resistant, and leak-proof container at the chairside. This container must be sealed and clearly labeled "QUARANTINE".
Quarantined items must not enter the general cleaning stream (e.g., shared ultrasonic baths). They must be cleaned and sterilised as a standalone load.

Separate Processing

Specialised Decontamination

Based on the risk (such as CJD), the practice may:

  • Use extended sterilisation cycles (e.g., 18 minutes at 134°C).
  • Engage an external facility for specialized decontamination.
  • Designate instruments as single-use and dispose of via high-temp incineration.
A detailed report must be completed, documenting the patient’s details, the batch numbers involved, and the specific actions taken.
